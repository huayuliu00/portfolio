Natural Materials as Learning Catalysts | Project Zero Art|Play Workshop with Boston Public Schools

In Spring 2024, I worked with Project Zero's Art|Play team to design and evaluate a "Natural Materials" professional development workshop for early childhood educators from Boston Public Schools. I co-developed interview protocols, transcribed and coded interviews with six PreK teachers and three cultural organization partners, and synthesized themes into an Art/Play codebook. I then helped organize and facilitate a hands-on professional development session—guiding 20+ PreK teachers, BPS curriculum designers, and community artists through kinesthetic activities that explored natural materials as learning catalysts. Throughout the workshop, I conducted field observations (photographing interactions, collecting participant reflections, and mapping engagement patterns) and logged artifacts into a shared repository. My mixed-methods analysis revealed that incorporating open-ended, nature-based prompts significantly enhanced teacher confidence in facilitating emergent, inquiry-driven play.

Formative Evaluation | Museum of Science

In Spring 2024, I led a formative evaluation of the Museum of Science's "Science in the Park" exhibit to inform the design of their upcoming "IHeartScience" exhibition. Partnering with a multidisciplinary team, I co-developed observation and interview protocols, secured ethical consent for both adult and child participants, and orchestrated data collection over three months. I conducted systematic field observations—timing visitor interactions, coding engagement themes, and mapping social behaviors—and facilitated exit interviews and surveys to capture family and caregiver perspectives. Through thematic analysis of 20 observation instances, 13 interviews, and 45 survey responses, I identified which interactive components (e.g., seesaw, lift, swing) fostered the highest levels of playful collaboration and social learning. My findings informed four key recommendations—expanding kinesthetic, intergenerational activities; enhancing accessibility and labeling; and incorporating staff-guided experiences—to ensure that "IHeartScience" will engage diverse audiences in meaningful, full-body exploration of STEM concepts.

Lunar New Year Celebration: Museum of Fine Arts x Josiah Quincy Elementary School

In February 2025, I collaborated with Josiah Quincy Elementary School to design and implement a bilingual Lunar New Year celebration that introduced K–5 students and their caregiver to Chinese calligraphy and cultural traditions. I crafted a multi-part lesson plan—including "Prosperity Characters" (福) writing, Xu Bin's Square Word calligraphy tutorials (for non-native Chinese participants), and "One Word for the Year" activities—by selecting age-appropriate materials and designing step-by-step instructions for both native and non-Chinese-writing participants.

Slow Looking at the Harvard Art Museum | Designing Reflective Engagement Tools for Visitors

In January 2024, I co-designed and facilitated a "Slow Looking" inquiry experience at the Harvard Art Museum, where our team created and iteratively refined a set of "Looking Cards" to guide visitors through reflective engagement with artworks. I led materials prototyping—composing card prompts, testing various sizes, and drafting accompanying handouts—and onboarded eight invited participants aged 23–30. During the 40-minute session, I observed individuals engaging with prompts, guided small-group discussions, and collected qualitative feedback through fieldnotes and participant reflections posted on a Padlet board. Analyzing 20 observation instances and pre/post reflections, I identified that the cards effectively slowed visitors' pacing, encouraged deeper connections between artworks, and scaffolded the development of personalized meaning.

Linguistics Thesis, Bryn Mawr College | Exploring Translanguaging in Mandarin and Japanese Classrooms

In Spring 2023, I conducted my senior Linguistics thesis on the use of translanguaging and code-switching in Mandarin and Japanese language classrooms to inform more inclusive foreign-language pedagogy. I designed a mixed-methods study—interviewing four professors (two Mandarin, two Japanese) and observing classroom interactions—to document when and why instructors shifted languages, how these practices aligned with institutional hierarchies, and professors' attitudes toward machine-translation tools. By coding observation instances and synthesizing insights from semi-structured interviews, I uncovered how targeted translanguaging strategies support scaffolding complex grammar and facilitate deeper comprehension for emergent bilinguals. My analysis led to evidence-based guidelines recommending a balanced integration of students' full linguistic repertoires, strategic use of English as metalanguage only when necessary, and thoughtful incorporation of translation software to bolster learner autonomy. These findings equip my teaching philosophy with practical, research-based techniques to design multilingual classrooms that honor student diversity and promote equitable access to the target language.

East Asian Languages and Cultures Thesis, Bryn Mawr College | A Case Study of Pu’an Temple

In Fall 2022, for my East Asian Languages and Cultures thesis at Bryn Mawr College, I examined how legal and political forces—from Qing-era edicts to modern SARA regulations and Japanese imperial policies—have shaped Pu'an Temple in northeastern China. I conducted archival research using the Pu'an Temple Manuscript and historical gazetteers, and performed thematic coding of documents and transcripts to trace the abbot's reincarnation lineage from 1632 to the present.

Culturally Responsive Mandarin Lesson | Designing for Multilingual Learners in Philadelphia’s Chinatown

In Fall 2022, I designed and taught a culturally responsive Mandarin lesson for middle schoolers at a charter school in Philadelphia’s Chinatown. Grounded in Understanding by Design principles, the lesson celebrated classroom diversity and supported emergent bilinguals. Drawing on pre-lesson surveys, I mapped students’ home countries and incorporated Pashto, Arabic, and Spanish translations alongside Chinese vocabulary to ensure accessibility for all learners. Guided by backwards-planned objectives, I facilitated partner-based activities where students practiced self-introductions both orally and visually, locating their home countries on a world map handout. I also provided teachers with a replicable, multimodal framework—including slides, handouts, and reflection prompts—to continue inclusive, culturally responsive language instruction beyond the session.
